Bunga Bunga, London, restaurant review

Bunga Bunga in Battersea, London is a packed pizzeria that will steal your heart , says Keith Miller.

4
Bunga Bunga, Battersea, London Photo: CLARA MOLDEN

By Keith Miller

7:00AM BST 13 Sep 2011

Comments

Bunga Bunga, 37 Battersea Bridge Road, London SW11 3BA,
Contact 020 7095 0360; www.bungabunga-london.com.
Price Dinner with wine, about £25 per head

You’ve got to hand it to Silvio Berlusconi. It’s not as if Italy’s many estimable virtues haven’t always gone hand in hand with a certain moustache-twirling naughtiness: a Machiavelli for every Michelangelo; for every soprano, a Soprano. But in a few scant years at the top, il Cavaliere has made over the Land of Beauty’s global reputation in his own image: a 74-year-old man with Velcro for hair and the complexion of a DFS sofa making suggestive grabbing gestures at a long chorus line of Zeppelin-chested, and often very young, women.

The British have long been susceptible to this kind of thing: witness the high camp of The Italian Job, and the enormous pepper grinders brandished at Pizza Express down the decades. But then we have also had a reverence for Italian food culture since Elizabeth David wrote about the savours of the warm south so solemnly, and so influentially, in the arid Fifties.

Bunga Bunga, recently opened on one of the trade routes into Chelsea from its lower-rent neighbour Battersea, and popular at once, is trying to have it both ways. It is what used to be called a “theme” restaurant – “an Englishman’s Italian”, says the rubric – with cartoonish Italiana on the walls: barmen dressed as gondoliers and cocktails served in little (not so little, to tell the truth) Fiat Cinquecentos, Colosseums and, in the case of a vaguely Negroni-esque house special, the gurning skull of the lubricious politico himself.

Waiters bustle around wearing those little earpieces that make them look as if they are auditioning for a Take That tribute band. Pony-tailed musicians croon songs of love, loss and Lambrusco at you; there’s a whiff of the burger joint in Pulp Fiction about the whole effect. It’s a million miles away from the pious slaughterhouse chic of the River Café, say; and yet the food is pretty good.

The mystical catalyst that makes this logistically and economically possible is the humble pizza. This is, on the surface, just some bread, cheese, tomato and other toppings. But you wouldn’t say Raphael’s Bridgewater Madonna is just a bit of cloth covered in linseed oil and turpentine with some mud and ground-up beetle mixed in.

We’ve all had yards of merely adequate pizza, and a certain amount of flat-out horrible pizza. This is because a crowded pizzeria is a relatively risk-free way to make money without highly trained chefs. And because it’s the sort of food we like if we’re hungry, or we have children with us, or we’re strapped for time, or (crucially) drunk.

But good pizza isn’t so much harder to make than bad – less so, certainly, than good steak, which simply costs much more wholesale, or good snail porridge, which needs someone who knows how to make it. You need a very hot oven and good ingredients. You need to get the seasoning and timing right, and Bobby Bacala’s your uncle.

At Bunga Bunga, the level of fun on offer (which won’t be everyone’s idea of fun – they have karaoke upstairs, for example, an activity which gives the phrase “extraordinary rendition” a whole new meaning) tends to nudge your expectations downwards in terms of the food you’ll be getting. And then the food nudges your expectations right up again.

Charcuterie and bruschetta arrive on little wooden boards in the currently imperative rustic style (I was half hoping they would come in china dishes modelled on the grand concavities of Ruby the Heartstealer’s bra). Little enamelled tin side plates are doled out for that Bicycle Thieves touch. The meats are good: cut a little thin maybe, but imaginatively chosen and presented (they have nduja, the fiery soft sausage from Sardinia; carpaccio is served with truffle aioli). “Polenta chips” – actually cubes – were hot, crisp and greaseless. We ordered an inky nero d’Avola from a shortish list. A girl with a Take That earpiece stopped by to ask us if everything was all right, and we said that it was.

Pizzas are Roman style, thinner and crisper than the real Neapolitan McCoy (to eat anything remotely like these in London you’ve got to go to Franco Manca in Brixton, Chiswick or, imminently, Stratford). My associate said he thought the dough was a bit “doughy”, but to me both our pizzas seemed well balanced and generously topped (a spree of salami for me, fresh and dried mushrooms for him). They are a bit smaller than at Pizza Express, but cheaper, too. Given that it’s apparently forbidden to go out in this part of town in groups of less than 15, the concept is presumably that a lot of sharing will go on.

Spurning ice cream (the only pudding on offer), we had two excellent espressos, the crema on top velvety, thick and the colour of Berlusconi’s bonce, the little drink beneath pungent, oily and caldo come cazzo, as they say in old Napoli. Our guy brought us free limoncellos, though there’s a long menu of amaros, grappas and suchlike. And then we were off, well fed, driven neither deaf nor insane. But I still think the name’s a hostage to fortune if Berlusconi does go down.
